Assignment 2

Sewing an originally designed

Jellycat Bouldering Chalk Bag

Prompt

A sewn chalk bag that remixes the “Jellycat” gift bag.

This prototype had to be made from flexible fabric or material, assembled using a sewing machine, and include a fastening mechanism such as Velcro, a drawstring, or a zipper. My project interprets those requirements through an originally designed “Jellycat” bouldering chalk bag.

Feature Review / Design

Motivation

I have always been very drawn to people making their own chalk bags, and at the same time, buying a cute one can be surprisingly expensive. To design my own bag, I started by looking at how other people made their chalk bags and learning from their experience. While searching for similar materials around my house, I found a small Jellycat bag that had the right scale and a curly surface that felt perfect as the outer layer of the chalk bag.

Design Logic

Reflecting on how I use current chalk bag designs, I identified two main pain points. First, chalk tends to get everywhere around the bag, so I often have to store it in a ziplock bag after every climb. Second, chalk can leak out easily when the bag is on the mat and gets deformed by pressure. To address that, I designed the bag with three inner layers for the chalk-holding system, including a middle ziplock-inspired layer sewn together with fabric. I originally wanted to sew an actual ziplock seal into the bag, but that plastic turned out to be too rigid for the sewing machine. Though the layered construction became quite thick, it taught me how to strategically stretch the fabric slightly and keep multiple layers aligned during sewing.

Analysis

What worked, what failed, and what each iteration taught me.

The first paper iteration did not go very far because I could not flip the prototype inside out for further development. The muslin iteration used four layers, and while I was trying to hide all of the seams neatly, I accidentally closed the innermost layer too early. Since the inner two layers were the same size, I ended up cutting out one of them so I could continue sewing the rest of the structure.

Another issue in the muslin prototype was that I sewed four layers together at once on two sides without realizing that the third layer needed to tighten independently with the drawstring. I learned that this could be solved by cutting that layer first, reducing its width, and then sewing it back so it could move independently while the other three layers stayed together. This iteration also taught me that every detail that belongs to an individual layer is easiest to complete first before combining all of the layers together, which made the final prototype much easier to assemble.

One unexpected issue in the final prototype was that the actual material thickness exceeded the sewing machine's capacity once all four layers were stacked together. I responded by machine-sewing three layers together and stitching the fourth layer by hand. That solution ended up being useful, because the outermost layer is mainly aesthetic rather than functional, so now it can be changed more easily when I want to try a different outer pattern.

Critique & Feedback

Feedback from class critique and climbing use.

In class, around six people responded very positively to the design and creativity of the chalk bag. One suggestion was to sew the inner and outer parts together more firmly, which I think makes sense because some of those connections had to be done manually due to sewing capacity limits. Another piece of feedback I found especially useful was the suggestion to place the two belt loops on the inner chalk bag rather than on the outer shell, since the outer shell is intended more as an aesthetic layer and is not attached as firmly.

I also brought the chalk bag to climbing and showed it to about two friends outside the class. They liked the design and appreciated how well the seams were hidden and aligned. Their main concern was that the drawstring closure was not fully sealed yet, so a small amount of chalk could still leak out.

Next steps

What I would improve in the next version.

In the next version, I would move the two loops to the inner part of the chalk bag, attach the aesthetic shell and inner bag more firmly, and adjust the drawstring closure position so that it can fully seal. Those changes would improve structural confidence while keeping the distinctive character of the design.
