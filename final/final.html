Schematic



The schematic outlines the connections for the input sensors and output components. The system takes input from a PIR motion sensor and a capacitive touch sensor. The PIR sensor is powered with 5V and connected to Arduino pin 5, with its digital output triggering detection. Sensitivity and delay time can be adjusted using the onboard potentiometers. Upon testing with cat user, PIR sensor is set to repeatable trigger mode, highest sensitivity and lowest delay time. The capacitive touch sensor, formed by an aluminum foil-covered enclosure, is connected between pins 12 and 8 with a 1MŒ© resistor to detect physical contact. For outputs, an LED strip is controlled using an N-FET MOSFET transistor. The Arduino sends a signal from pin 9 to the transistor gate, switching the LED strip on and off via the drain-source connection with a 12V power supply. Additionally, an active buzzer is connected to pin 10 through a 220Œ© resistor to limit current. When triggered, the Arduino activates the buzzer and LED strip as deterrents.





Firmware



The firmware for this system is carefully designed for flexibility and reusability. All components are secured using non-conductive tape, allowing for easy adjustments and modifications without damaging the setup. Instead of permanent soldering, the connections rely on the natural tension and elasticity of the wires, ensuring a firm grip in the breadboard and component terminals even when the enclosure is upside down. The wiring is strategically arranged to remain stable while minimizing clutter. Some connections are hidden within the side flaps of the cardboard box, maintaining a clean and organized layout.
