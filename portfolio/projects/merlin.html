Merlin Bird ID: help young novice birdwatchers feel less overwhelmed and more engaged in the field.

In a team of four, I led UX research and product management for HCDE 25' major capstone with the Cornell Lab of Ornithology and UW Ecofutures Lab. We studied what it actually means to be a young novice birder, then translated that research into product directions Merlin could use to support confidence, learning, and long-term engagement.

Role

UX Research Lead & Product Manager (Team size: 4)

Partners

Cornell Lab of Ornithology & UW Ecofutures Lab

Timeline

Jan–Jun 2025

Scope

Beginner birding research, product strategy, concept directions, and user testing

Overview

Designing for beginners meant looking beyond identification accuracy.

Merlin Bird ID already helps millions of users identify birds, but the team at Cornell wanted to better understand younger novice birdwatchers: what motivates them, where they get stuck, and what keeps them engaged long enough to keep learning.

Design question: How might Merlin Bird ID act as a companion app to young novice birdwatchers aged 18 to 40, encouraging regular engagement and longer-term environmentally conscious lifestyles?

My contribution centered on structuring the research, guiding synthesis, and turning that evidence into product directions the team could defend.

My Role

Led the research direction

I helped frame what we needed to learn about novice birders, keeping the project focused on motivations, barriers, identity, and real-world field behavior instead of only app usability.

Drove product management

I connected sponsor goals, team scope, and research evidence into a coherent product story, helping the team prioritize opportunities that were meaningful for Merlin rather than just interesting observations.

Translated findings into design action

I helped synthesize the work into actionable implications for onboarding, confidence-building, learning support, and engagement so our final concepts stayed grounded in user needs.

Research

A mixed-method study built a grounded picture of young novice birders.

We worked in phases, beginning with surveys, field observations, and media review to build a broad understanding of the audience. From there, we conducted in-depth interviews with novice birders to understand their histories, habits, tools, and pain points in context.

The survey helped us recruit and compare participants using measures like bird interest, knowledge, frequency, and time spent birdwatching. We then used grounded theory methods, transcript coding, thematic analysis, and affinity mapping to convert raw data into recurring patterns the team could use.

Findings

01

Birding is often spontaneous and social

Many novices encounter birds during daily life rather than planned outings, and their entry into birding often happens through friends or shared moments.

02

There is no single novice journey

People come to birding through different identities, motivations, and levels of commitment, so Merlin cannot assume a single path from beginner to expert.

03

Barriers go beyond app UI

Time, tool access, knowledge gaps, information overload, weather, and social dynamics all shape how confident or discouraged a novice birder feels.

What We Learned

Confidence depends on context, identity, and support systems.

One of the clearest research outcomes was that novice birdwatchers do not simply need better identification output. They need support that matches their level of knowledge, available tools, and social situation. Many participants did not even identify with the term “birdwatcher,” which matters for how a product frames itself and invites learning.

We also found that community plays a major role in identity and learning. Some novices want encouragement and conversation, while others feel intimidated by more experienced birders and fear getting identifications wrong. That made confidence-building a product and community design problem, not just an interface problem.

Implications

Design for learning, not just results

For many novices, identification is not the end goal. It is one step in learning more about birds, so Merlin should explain, reassure, and guide instead of only returning an answer.

Support users with different levels of identity

Not every target user calls themselves a birdwatcher. Product language and onboarding should meet people where they are instead of assuming strong prior interest or expertise.

Make engagement flexible and low-pressure

Because many novice users bird spontaneously and without specialized tools, the most helpful features reduce overwhelm and fit into everyday moments rather than demanding deep commitment.

Design Response

We translated the research into product directions Merlin could test.

Our final recommendations focused on three opportunity areas: motivation and habit building, learning birding basics, and social sharing. Instead of treating beginner support as a single onboarding screen, we framed it as an ongoing system of confidence-building touchpoints.

The team developed potential design solutions grounded in the research, then tested those ideas with users to understand which concepts felt most useful and resonant for different novice types.

Outcome

A research-led product direction for supporting novice birders.

The project delivered a stronger shared definition of who novice birdwatchers are, what barriers shape their experience, and where Merlin could better support them through product decisions. It also gave the team a set of potential design solutions rooted in evidence rather than assumptions.

For me, this capstone was a chance to lead research and product framing together: defining the learning agenda, helping the team synthesize large amounts of qualitative data, and turning those findings into actionable design and product recommendations.
